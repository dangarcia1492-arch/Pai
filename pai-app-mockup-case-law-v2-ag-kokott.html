What was decided

Opinion: Hungary failed to fulfil obligations under EU law

On 12 February 2026, AG Juliane Kokott delivered her Opinion in Commission v Hungary (Protection against foreign political interference), recommending the Court find Hungary's Law LXXXVIII of 2023 on the protection of national sovereignty infringes EU primary and secondary law, the Charter, and the GDPR. The Opinion is non-binding; the Court will deliver its own judgment in due course.

What was at stake

Sovereignty Protection Office: powers and reach

The contested law establishes a Sovereignty Protection Office empowered to investigate organisations and persons carrying out activities "in the interest of other States and foreign actors". The Office can compel disclosure (including personal data), forward findings to other authorities, and publish accusatory annual reports. Its investigative powers are not subject to judicial review.

Kokott found these powers indirectly discriminate against foreign service providers and breach the free movement of information society services, the free movement of capital, and the Services Directive. The processing of personal data — including data revealing political opinions — engages Articles 9 and 10 of the GDPR; the absence of safeguards renders that processing unlawful.

What this means

First Article 2 TEU action coupled with GDPR breaches

If the Court adopts Kokott's reasoning, the judgment will bind all Member States and force Hungary to dismantle or substantially reform the Office. The case is the first time the Commission has invoked breaches of Article 2 TEU values alongside concrete GDPR and Charter violations in a single infringement action — a precedent for future rule-of-law actions where data-protection breaches are part of a wider pattern.
