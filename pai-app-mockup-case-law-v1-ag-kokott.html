AG Kokott: Hungary's Sovereignty Protection Office breaches EU law
European Union · Hungary · 12 February 2026 · Privacy AI

Advocate General Juliane Kokott proposes that the Court of Justice find Hungary infringed EU law by adopting Law LXXXVIII of 2023, which establishes a Sovereignty Protection Office with broad investigative powers and no judicial review. Non-binding Opinion; Court judgment to follow.

What was decided

Opinion: Hungary failed to fulfil obligations under EU law

On 12 February 2026, AG Juliane Kokott delivered her Opinion in Commission v Hungary (Protection against foreign political interference), recommending the Court of Justice find that Hungary's Law LXXXVIII of 2023 on the protection of national sovereignty infringes several provisions of EU primary and secondary law, the Charter of Fundamental Rights, and the GDPR.

The Opinion is non-binding; the Court of Justice will deliver its own judgment in due course. The Commission's infringement action under Article 258 TFEU was lodged on 19 December 2024.

What was at stake

Sovereignty Protection Office: powers and reach

The contested Hungarian law establishes an independent Sovereignty Protection Office empowered to investigate organisations and individuals carrying out activities "in the interest of other States and foreign actors". The Office can compel information disclosure (including personal data), forward findings to other authorities, and publish accusatory or stigmatising annual reports. Its investigative powers are not subject to judicial review.

Kokott found the Office's powers indirectly discriminate against foreign service providers, breach the free movement of information society services (Directive 2000/31), the free movement of capital, and the Services Directive (2006/123). The processing of personal data — including data revealing political opinions — engages Articles 9 and 10 of the GDPR; the absence of safeguards renders that processing unlawful.

What this means

Binding precedent if confirmed by the Court

If the Court adopts Kokott's reasoning, the judgment will bind all EU Member States and require Hungary to dismantle or substantially reform the Sovereignty Protection Office. The case is the first time the Commission has invoked breaches of Article 2 TEU values alongside concrete GDPR and Charter violations in a single infringement action — a precedent for future rule-of-law actions where data-protection breaches are part of a wider pattern.
