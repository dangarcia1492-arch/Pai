C-829/24 · ECLI:EU:C:2026:——— · Commission v Hungary (Protection against foreign political interference)

AG Kokott: Hungary's Sovereignty Protection Office breaches EU law

European Union · Hungary · 12 February 2026 · Privacy AI

Advocate General Juliane Kokott proposes the Court of Justice find Hungary infringed EU law by adopting Law LXXXVIII of 2023, which establishes a Sovereignty Protection Office with broad investigative powers and no judicial oversight.

1 · Background

Hungary's Sovereignty Protection Office and the Commission's infringement action

In December 2023, Hungary adopted Law LXXXVIII of 2023 on the protection of national sovereignty. The law establishes an independent Sovereignty Protection Office empowered to identify organisations or individuals carrying out activities "in the interest of other States and foreign actors". The Office can compel disclosure of information (including personal data), forward findings to other authorities, and publish accusatory annual reports. Its investigative powers are not subject to judicial review.

On 19 December 2024, the European Commission lodged an action for failure to fulfil obligations against Hungary under Article 258 TFEU, alleging breaches of fundamental freedoms, the Charter of Fundamental Rights, the GDPR, and — for the first time in an infringement action — the EU's foundational values under Article 2 TEU. The hearing took place on 19 November 2025; AG Kokott delivered her Opinion on 12 February 2026; the Court's judgment is pending.

2 · Legal basis

Provisions invoked

The Commission and AG Kokott rely on a combination of EU primary and secondary law:

Primary law: Article 2 TEU (values of the Union); Articles 1, 7, 11, 21 of the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union (dignity, private life, expression, non-discrimination); Article 8(2) Charter (data protection).
Internal-market freedoms: Article 3(2) e-Commerce Directive 2000/31/EC; Articles 16 and 19 Services Directive 2006/123/EC.
Data protection: Articles 9 and 10 GDPR (special-category data and data on criminal convictions).

The contested national instrument is Law LXXXVIII of 2023 on the protection of national sovereignty (Hungary).

3 · Ruling

AG Opinion: Hungary infringed EU law in several respects

On 12 February 2026, AG Juliane Kokott proposed that the Court of Justice find Hungary failed to fulfil its obligations under EU law. She concluded the Office's investigative powers and disclosure obligations indirectly discriminate against foreign service providers and breach the free movement of information society services, the free movement of capital, and the Services Directive. The Office's processing of personal data — including data revealing political opinions — engages Articles 9 and 10 GDPR; absence of safeguards renders that processing unlawful.

The AG Opinion is non-binding; the Court of Justice will deliver its judgment in due course. If the Court adopts Kokott's reasoning, the ruling will bind all EU Member States and require Hungary to dismantle or substantially reform the Office. The Commission stated this is the first infringement action invoking Article 2 TEU values alongside concrete GDPR and Charter violations in a single action.
