Interactive pattern explorations

Five candidates for extending the EU + US tracker's pin-and-pick interaction across the platform. Two variants per candidate — each pair is intentionally different in mental model, not just visual style. Each variant asks: what is the user's actual job here, and what's the most satisfying interaction for that job?

1

News — Daily Brief

pai-app-news.html

The job: A practitioner wakes up Monday and has 15 minutes before standup. She wants to skim what regulators did over the weekend, save what's worth reading deeper, send 2-3 to her team. She is not doing comparative analysis — she's doing curation under time pressure.

Variant A

Stories pinboard + AI-written digest intro

Tick stories → they pin at the top as compact cards. Click Generate digest → Claude writes a 2-paragraph intro tying them together. Output: a single shareable "Monday brief".

Variant B

Reading-list sequencer (drag-to-tray, time-budgeted)

Drag stories into a right-side tray. Tray shows estimated reading time per story, with a running total. Re-order by drag. Schedule delivery. Share read-only link. Like a custom magazine, not a digest.

Why these differ: A treats the output as one document (the digest). B treats the output as a queue (the reading session). A optimises for sharing; B optimises for personal triage. Built side-by-side they cover different days of the same user's week.
2

Authorities — Regulator shortlist

pai-app-authorities.html

The job: An in-house counsel at a multinational is preparing a cross-border data flow assessment. She needs to compare the UK's ICO, Germany's BfDI, France's CNIL and Ireland's DPC — not the full 246 DPAs, just these four — across activity, enforcement intensity, and recent guidance. She's building input for a board memo.

Variant A

Compare-table (column-per-authority)

Tick 2–5 authorities → 4th tab opens a row-keyed comparison table. Each authority is a column; rows are the dimensions a board memo needs. Copy-paste-ready into Word.

3

Case Law — Casebook builder

pai-app-case-law.html

The job: A litigator is preparing for a hearing on Article 22 GDPR (automated decision-making). He needs Schrems I, Bosphorus, and three German Federal Court rulings in a single bundle to file with the court — and he needs to know which cases argue against his position so he can address them in his brief.

Variant A

Vertical bundle (court-ready PDF)

Tick cases → stacked view with each case's Background · Legal basis · Ruling sections expanded, instrument cross-links inline. Send to client / file with court / print as bundle.

Variant B

Citation graph (cited-by / distinguishes / overrules)

Pick Schrems I → graph view. Nodes = cases. Edges = "cited by", "distinguishes", "overrules". Toggle "What argues against this?" to highlight distinguishing cases in red. Click any node to add to bundle.

Variant B · Citation graph

  • "What argues against this?" — the question litigators actually ask, surfaced as a toggle
  • Graph → bundle — clicking a node both highlights its relations AND adds it to the case bundle on the right
  • Depth control — 1-hop / 2-hop / 3-hop reach (avoid overwhelming graph)
Why these differ: A is output-first (the user knows what they want; deliver the bundle). B is discovery-first (the user knows their position; show them what they need to confront). A litigator uses B before drafting, then A after.
4

Legislation — Cite stack

pai-app-legislation.html

The job: An academic is writing a comparative article: "Profiling rules across GDPR Art 22, UK DPA s.14, Brazil LGPD Art 20, and California CCPA §1798.185." She needs the article text of each clause side-by-side, AND she needs the citations formatted for her journal (Bluebook).

Variant A

Side-by-side article view (clause-level parallel reading)

Tick instruments → pick specific articles within each → 4th tab shows them as parallel columns. Original-language toggle (per the house style rule on verbatim titles). Cross-references hyperlinked.

Variant B

Memo builder (citation style + .docx export)

Pick instruments + articles → assemble into a memo template. Pick citation style (Bluebook / OSCOLA / AGPS / plain). Preview shows formatted citations. Copy to clipboard for Word or download .docx — integrates with the user's writing tool, not the platform.

5

Home page — Today's brief

pai-home-private.html

The job: The user lands on the home page first thing in the morning. The home page is the trailhead — they're not doing focused work, they're orienting. The question is: can the home page do enough that they don't need to navigate elsewhere?

Variant A

Manual curation card ("Build my brief")

Top-of-home widget. Tick items from the news ticker, regulatory updates, watchlist hits → the widget transforms into your personal brief card. Save as bookmark, schedule for email delivery.

Variant A · Manual curation

Tick the items you want in your morning summary.
CNIL fines Apple €8M

South Korea AI guidance
Build brief · 2 items →
  • Trailhead UX — same tick interaction the user knows from News + Trackers
  • Recurring brief — "Send me this curation every weekday at 8am"
  • Continuity — picks up where they left off via localStorage
Variant B

Triage assistant ("Since you were last here, 14 things changed")

System-curated. The home page surfaces what's changed since last login — scoped to the user's home jurisdiction + watchlist. Each item has Yes / No / Snooze. Yes pins to a personal board. No archives. Snooze defers. The user triages rather than curates.

Why these differ: A is active curation (the user knows what they want). B is passive triage (the system proposes, the user filters). A treats the user as expert; B treats the system as expert. The right answer probably depends on the user's seniority — a junior in-house counsel benefits from B; a partner benefits from A. Could co-exist.

How to ship this without rebuilding 5×

Each variant pair runs on the same underlying widget primitive — selectable rows + pinned-summary + saved-list — but customised per surface. The right move is to extract pai-compare-widget.js + pai-compare-widget.css as a shared module that each page imports and configures (data-source selector, "what does the comparison view render" callback, localStorage prefix, share-URL encoder). Then EU + US (already built) + News + Authorities + Case Law + Legislation + Home = one widget, seven surfaces.
