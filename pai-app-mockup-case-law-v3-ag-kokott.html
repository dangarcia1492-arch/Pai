AG Kokott: Hungary's Sovereignty Protection Office breaches EU law

C-829/24 · CJEU AG Opinion · 12 February 2026 · European Union · Hungary · Privacy AI Pending
1 Decision

AG proposes finding of infringement against Hungary

On 12 February 2026, AG Juliane Kokott delivered her Opinion in Commission v Hungary (Protection against foreign political interference), recommending the Court of Justice find that Hungary's Law LXXXVIII of 2023 on the protection of national sovereignty infringes EU primary and secondary law, the Charter of Fundamental Rights, and the GDPR. The Opinion is non-binding; Court judgment to follow.

2 Reasoning

Sovereignty Protection Office: powers without judicial oversight

The contested law establishes a Sovereignty Protection Office empowered to investigate organisations and persons "in the interest of foreign States and actors". The Office can compel disclosure (including personal data), forward findings to other authorities, and publish accusatory annual reports. Its investigative powers are not subject to judicial review.

"The powers conferred on the Office are, in part, not proportionate to the legitimate aim of protecting domestic democratic discourse and processes."

Kokott found these powers indirectly discriminate against foreign service providers and breach the free movement of information society services (Directive 2000/31), the free movement of capital, and the Services Directive. The processing of personal data — including data revealing political opinions — engages Articles 9 and 10 of the GDPR; absence of safeguards renders that processing unlawful.

3 Effect

First Article 2 TEU action coupled with GDPR breaches

If the Court adopts Kokott's reasoning, the judgment will bind all EU Member States and require Hungary to dismantle or substantially reform the Office. The case is the first time the Commission has invoked breaches of Article 2 TEU values alongside concrete GDPR and Charter violations in a single infringement action — a precedent for future rule-of-law actions where data-protection breaches are part of a wider pattern.
